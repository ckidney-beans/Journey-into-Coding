# My Keyboard Odyssey: From Normie to Nerd

## The Slippery Slope

Like every self-respecting geek, I've finally tumbled down the infamous rabbit hole of custom keyboards. I suppose my gateway drug was the Razer Blackwidow RGB—my first mechanical keyboard and a solid gaming companion. It served me well, though it was roughly the size of a small aircraft carrier. That behemoth still lives on my sim racing rig, where space constraints are clearly not a concern.

But why do we geeks obsess over custom keyboards anyway? For me, it's partly sensory—that satisfying _click-clack_ that makes coworkers shoot dirty looks across the office. But let's be honest: it's mostly about the flex. There's something deeply satisfying about owning a typing instrument that's uniquely yours, meticulously customized down to the sound profile of each keystroke. It's mechanical keyboard ASMR, and I'm here for it.

## The Keyboard Journey Continues

My journey progressed to an RK 65% wireless keyboard, which became my work daily driver since January 2022. Small enough to slip into my bag without requiring physiotherapy afterward, it was a solid performer.

Just a couple of weeks ago, I leveled up to an Epomaker X Aula 75. This reasonably priced beauty made me realize just how subpar the keys on my cheaper RK keyboard actually were. Typing on this thing is like driving a luxury car after years in a beater—suddenly you realize what you've been missing. The sound! The feel! It's currently assigned to my gaming/sim racing setup, where it's living its best life.

## The End Game!? (Narrator: It's Never Really the End Game)

And now, we arrive at my latest acquisition—the YMDK SZR36. This keyboard showed up from China in a mysterious unmarked cardboard box, with exactly zero documentation. For the uninitiated who might wonder why a keyboard needs a manual: this isn't just any keyboard. It's this bizarre contraption:

Switching to this keyboard has been... an adventure. Like many in my generation, I never properly learned to touch type. Sure, I can type without looking at the keyboard, but I've never used the "correct" fingers for each key, and I've never been setting any speed records.

So naturally, I've decided to torture myself further by practicing typing THREE times a day for the next 30 days, meticulously recording my progress (or lack thereof). Current results are not exactly LinkedIn-worthy:

I'm using a website called [monkeytype](monkeytype.com) to track my journey from keyboard catastrophe to... well, hopefully something better.

## Why Am I Doing This To Myself?

Will this keyboard become my daily driver? The jury's still out. I'm honestly shocked at how challenging it's been to adapt to just the split design alone—and that's without switching to a more efficient keyboard layout, which I had briefly (and perhaps insanely) considered.

If you noticed in the photo, this keyboard isn't just split—the entire key layout is different. Standard keyboards have staggered keys, a ridiculous legacy feature carried over from mechanical typewriters where it prevented the metal arms from jamming. Modern digital keyboards don't need this, but we kept it anyway because... tradition?

My split keyboard has linear keys, which means all my carefully cultivated muscle memory for common words is now useless. My fingers keep landing where keys used to be, leading to some truly creative typos and occasional muttered profanity.

So why am I putting myself through this keyboard bootcamp? I'd like to claim it's all about ergonomics and health, but honestly, it's mostly because it looks cool. There's something satisfying about using a device that makes normal people ask, "What in the world is THAT?" when they see your desk.

That said, I've noticed an unexpected benefit—my posture has improved. With the split design, my hands no longer need to crowd together, allowing my shoulders to open up naturally. I find myself sitting straighter instead of in my usual "tech goblin" hunch.

And there's the legitimate health angle. Many people turn to split keyboards after developing repetitive strain injuries. I've had occasional bouts of what feels like tennis elbow, which I initially blamed on my sim racing setup. However, it recently flared up during a period of heavy laptop use with no racing involved.

So this strange keyboard experiment is partly an investigation: could this odd-looking contraption actually be healthier for long-term use? Only time (and my typing test results) will tell. In the meantime, I'll be over here trying to remember which layer contains the question mark.
